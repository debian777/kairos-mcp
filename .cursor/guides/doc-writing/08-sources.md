# Sources and Further Reading

This guide synthesizes principles from technical writing, cognitive science, design, and journalism. Every recommendation is backed by research or decades of proven practice.

---

## Core Books

### Writing and Style

**"The Elements of Style"** by William Strunk Jr. and E.B. White (1959)
- The foundational text on clear writing
- "Omit needless words" (Rule 17)
- Active voice, parallel structure, definite language
- Short (105 pages), essential reading

**"On Writing Well"** by William Zinsser (1976)
- The craft of non-fiction writing
- Clarity, simplicity, brevity, humanity
- "Writing is thinking on paper"
- Read: Chapters 2-6 (Principles), Chapter 12 (Writing About Technology)

**"Politics and the English Language"** by George Orwell (1946)
- Essay on clarity vs. obfuscation
- Six rules for clear writing
- Why language matters
- Free online, read in 30 minutes

**"Writing Tools: 55 Essential Strategies for Every Writer"** by Roy Peter Clark (2006)
- Practical techniques for better writing
- Energy in verbs, front-loading, sentence length
- Each tool is actionable
- Read: Tools 1-20 (fundamentals)

### Technical Writing

**"Docs Like Code"** by Anne Gentle (2017)
- Treating documentation as a software artifact
- Docs in version control, tested, reviewed
- Continuous documentation
- Essential for modern documentation practice

**"Living Documentation"** by Cyrille Martraire (2019)
- Documentation that evolves with code
- Single source of truth
- Automated documentation strategies
- Read: Part I (Philosophy), Part II (Practices)

**"The Art of Explanation"** by Lee LeFever (2012)
- How to make complex ideas understandable
- Explanation design patterns
- Context → story → application
- Read: Chapters 5-8 (The Art)

### Information Architecture

**"Information Architecture"** by Louis Rosenfeld, Peter Morville, Jorge Arango (4th ed., 2015)
- How to organize information for findability
- Navigation patterns, taxonomy, search
- The foundation of structured documentation
- Read: Chapters 1-4 (Fundamentals), Chapter 9 (Organization Systems)

**"Information Anxiety"** by Richard Saul Wurman (1989)
- Why information overwhelms
- How to present information clearly
- Five ways to organize information: category, time, location, alphabet, continuum
- Dated but foundational

### Design and Visual Hierarchy

**"The Visual Display of Quantitative Information"** by Edward Tufte (1983)
- Data-ink ratio: maximize information, minimize clutter
- "Clutter and confusion are failures of design"
- Visual clarity principles
- Read: Chapter 4 (Data-Ink), Chapter 5 (Chartjunk)

**"The Non-Designer's Design Book"** by Robin Williams (4th ed., 2014)
- Four principles: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity (CRAP)
- Visual hierarchy for non-designers
- Typography basics
- Accessible introduction to design

**"Presentation Zen"** by Garr Reynolds (2008)
- Simplicity in visual design
- Whitespace as a design element
- Less is more
- Originally for slides, applies to all visual communication

### Usability and User Experience

**"Don't Make Me Think"** by Steve Krug (2000, 3rd ed. 2014)
- Web usability principles
- How people actually use documentation
- Scanning vs. reading
- Essential, short (200 pages), read in one sitting

**"Letting Go of the Words"** by Ginny Redish (2nd ed., 2012)
- Writing for the web (applies to all digital documentation)
- How people scan and read online
- Organizing for findability
- Read: Chapters 2-6

**"Rocket Surgery Made Easy"** by Steve Krug (2010)
- Do-it-yourself usability testing
- Watch real people use your documentation
- Find what doesn't work
- Read: Entire book (short, practical)

### Cognitive Science

**"Made to Stick"** by Chip Heath and Dan Heath (2007)
- Why some ideas survive and others die
- SUCCESs framework: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories
- How to make documentation memorable
- Read: Introduction + Chapter 1 (Simple)

**"The Sense of Style"** by Steven Pinker (2014)
- The science of good writing
- Curse of knowledge (why experts write badly)
- Classic style (clarity through directness)
- Read: Chapter 3 (Curse of Knowledge), Chapter 4 (Web, Tree, String)

**"100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People"** by Susan Weinschenk (2011)
- Cognitive psychology for designers
- How people perceive, think, and remember
- Attention limits, cognitive load
- Read: Sections on attention and memory

## Academic Papers

**"The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two"** by George A. Miller (1956)
- Working memory limits
- Why chunking works
- Foundation of cognitive load theory
- [PDF widely available online]

**"Cognitive Load Theory"** by John Sweller (1988)
- Intrinsic, extraneous, and germane cognitive load
- Implications for information design
- Why simple is better
- Search: "Sweller cognitive load theory instructional design"

**"Dual Coding Theory"** by Allan Paivio (1971, 1986)
- Verbal and visual processing channels
- Why diagrams + text > text alone
- Multimedia learning foundation
- Search: "Paivio dual coding theory"

**"F-Shaped Pattern for Reading Web Content"** by Jakob Nielsen (2006)
- Eye-tracking study results
- How people actually scan
- Design implications
- [Free on Nielsen Norman Group website]

**"How Users Read on the Web"** by Jakob Nielsen (1997)
- Users scan, don't read
- 79% of users scan, only 16% read word-by-word
- Implications for writing
- [Free on Nielsen Norman Group website]

**"The Minimalist Instruction Approach"** by John Carroll (1990)
- Less is more in documentation
- Task-oriented, error-recovery focus
- Foundation of modern technical writing
- Search: "Carroll minimalist instruction"

**"Why is Software Documentation Important?"** — Multiple studies
- Documentation quality correlates with software quality
- Lack of documentation is #1 complaint in OSS
- Search: "software documentation importance research"

## Web Resources

### Guidelines and Standards

**Diátaxis Framework** — https://diataxis.fr/
- Four documentation types: tutorial, how-to, reference, explanation
- Clear distinction between types
- The foundation for organizing documentation

**Plain Language Guidelines** — plainlanguage.gov
- US government guidelines for clear writing
- Directly applicable to technical writing
- Actionable checklist

**W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)** — w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/quickref/
- Accessibility for documentation
- Header hierarchy, alt text, color contrast
- Legal requirement in many contexts

**Keep a Changelog** — keepachangelog.com
- Standard for changelog format
- Guiding principles, format spec
- Widely adopted

**Conventional Commits** — conventionalcommits.org
- Commit message standard
- Enables automated changelog generation
- Used with semantic versioning

### Style Guides

**Google Developer Documentation Style Guide** — developers.google.com/style
- Comprehensive technical writing style guide
- Word list, formatting, accessibility
- Actively maintained

**Microsoft Writing Style Guide** — learn.microsoft.com/style-guide/
- Modern technical writing style
- Conversational, accessible
- Updated for cloud era

**GitLab Documentation Style Guide** — docs.gitlab.com/ee/development/documentation/styleguide/
- Example of opinionated, consistent style
- Markdown conventions
- Used by large distributed team

### Communities and Blogs

**Nielsen Norman Group** — nngroup.com
- Usability research and guidelines
- Articles on web writing, information architecture
- Evidence-based

**Write the Docs** — writethedocs.org
- Global community of documentarians
- Conferences, meetups, Slack
- Job board, learning resources

**I'd Rather Be Writing** — idratherbewriting.com (Tom Johnson)
- Technical writing blog
- API documentation, tools, careers
- Practical advice

## Recommended Reading Order

### If You Read Three Books
1. "Don't Make Me Think" — Krug (understand how people read)
2. "The Elements of Style" — Strunk & White (write clearly)
3. "Docs Like Code" — Gentle (documentation practice)

### If You Read Ten Books
Add:
4. "On Writing Well" — Zinsser (craft of writing)
5. "Information Architecture" — Rosenfeld et al. (structure)
6. "Letting Go of the Words" — Redish (web writing)
7. "Made to Stick" — Heath & Heath (memorable ideas)
8. "Living Documentation" — Martraire (docs that evolve)
9. "The Non-Designer's Design Book" — Williams (visual hierarchy)
10. "The Sense of Style" — Pinker (why experts write badly)

### If You're Going Deep
Add everything above, plus:
- Tufte on visualization
- Orwell on language
- Nielsen papers on usability
- Paivio and Sweller on cognitive load

## Quick Reference: Key Concepts

| Concept | Source | Key Takeaway |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Omit needless words | Strunk & White | Every word must earn its space |
| Inverted pyramid | Journalism | Most important information first |
| F-pattern | Nielsen | People scan left and top |
| Cognitive load | Sweller | Minimize extraneous mental effort |
| Dual coding | Paivio | Visual + verbal > verbal alone |
| Curse of knowledge | Pinker | Experts forget what it's like not to know |
| Progressive disclosure | Cooper | Layer information (don't dump all at once) |
| One document, one purpose | Tufte | Focus on single goal |
| Data-ink ratio | Tufte | Maximize signal, minimize noise |
| SUCCESs | Heath & Heath | Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories |
| Minimalist instruction | Carroll | Task-oriented, error-recovery focused |
| Plain language | US Gov | Use simple words, active voice, short sentences |
| Diátaxis | Procida | Four doc types: tutorial, how-to, reference, explanation |

## What This Guide Doesn't Cover

- Video documentation
- Interactive tutorials
- Localization and translation
- Documentation-as-code toolchains (CI/CD for docs)
- AI-assisted documentation (prompting, generation)
- API documentation specifics (OpenAPI, etc.)

For these topics, see:
- **API documentation:** "Documenting APIs" by Tom Johnson (idratherbewriting.com)
- **Docs-as-code tools:** "Modern Technical Writing" by Andrew Etter
- **Localization:** Write the Docs resources on internationalization
